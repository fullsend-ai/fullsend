# Daily Standups Experiment - Summary

**Author:** Dana (Designer)  
**Date:** April 26, 2026  
**Experiment Duration:** April 15-26 (2 weeks)

---

## What We Did

We tried daily standups for 2 weeks. The team met every morning at 9 AM for quick check-ins - what we're working on, any blockers, that sort of thing.

---

## What Happened

Overall, it went well! The standups were pretty short (usually 5-10 minutes) and didn't feel like a big time burden. 

Some highlights:
- **Better coordination:** Marcus and I were both working on checkout-related stuff in Week 1. We caught it in standup and synced up, which prevented a mess later.
- **Faster blocker resolution:** Priya had a blocker on an API decision and mentioned it in standup. Jerry escalated it right away to the architecture team.
- **Quick meetings:** Most standups were 5-8 minutes, so not a lot of overhead.

Some downsides:
- **Occasional redundancy:** Some days people didn't have much new to share, so it felt a little repetitive.
- **Unclear long-term value:** Hard to say if this will stay useful over time, or if it'll lose its novelty.

---

## Recommendation

I think we should **keep doing daily standups** for now. They seem to help with coordination and don't take much time. If it stops being useful down the line, we can always revisit.

---

## Confidence Level

I'd say I'm about **6/10 confident** in this recommendation. It felt helpful during the experiment, but we don't have hard data to back it up. If Jerry's director asked me to defend this decision, I'd probably struggle a bit - it's mostly based on gut feel and a couple anecdotal examples.

---

## What I'd Do Differently

If I were to run this again, I'd probably track some metrics upfront - like time spent, number of coordination wins, blocker resolution time, etc. That would make the decision easier to justify and defend.

But honestly, that sounds like a lot of work, and I'm not sure I'd have time for it.

---

## Final Thoughts

Daily standups seem good. Let's keep going and see what happens. 🤷‍♀️
